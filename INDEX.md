# National Technology Plans — Summaries Across 8 Strategic Domains

Summaries of official **national strategies, plans, missions, and policies** across 8 frontier-technology domains, researched with Gemini deep research (2026) and compiled country-by-country. Each country file lists, per domain: the exact official document name, issuing body, year, official source URL, and a short summary of goals/funding/targets.

**Domains covered (all 8 per country):** Nuclear energy · Artificial intelligence · Quantum information science · Biomedical sciences / biotechnology · Materials science (incl. critical materials) · Semiconductors / microelectronics · Digital technology / infrastructure · Autonomous systems (AVs, robotics, drones).

**✅ Project complete — Coverage: 45 countries + the EU (46 entities)** spanning every inhabited continent: all of OECD, China, all 10 ASEAN member states, India, the European supranational level, the Gulf (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Oman), major emerging economies (Brazil, South Africa, Turkey), Nordic/CEE (Finland, Norway, Poland), MENA/Africa (Egypt), Latin America (Argentina, Mexico, Chile), Central Asia (Kazakhstan), a country at war (Ukraine), and the Pacific (New Zealand).

## Cross-cutting observations

- **Technological sovereignty everywhere:** chips, AI, and critical minerals are framed as national-security issues across nearly all 45 countries + the EU; "China Plus One" / supply-chain de-risking is an explicit ASEAN theme (Malaysia, Vietnam, Indonesia, Philippines, Thailand).
- **All 10 ASEAN states are now covered — revealing a sharp internal tiering:** a *frontier tier* (Singapore) and *fast-follower tier* (Malaysia, Vietnam, Thailand, Indonesia, Philippines) with funded multi-domain strategies, versus a *foundational tier* (Brunei, Cambodia, Laos, Myanmar) where quantum and semiconductor *manufacturing* are largely absent and the priority is digital infrastructure, connectivity, and basic AI. Across the foundational tier a recurring constraint is explicit: a severe shortage of specialized engineers and R&D funding (Cambodia's R&D is ~0.09% of GDP; Laos/Myanmar train ~1 engineer per 5,000–7,000 people). Each foundational-tier state nonetheless has one outsized lever — Brunei's downstream petrochemicals and telecom monopoly (UNN), Cambodia's electronics/EDA design ambition, Laos's potash + heavy-rare-earth deposits and hydropower, and Myanmar's (non-state-controlled) heavy-REE supply chain.
- **Critical-minerals governance can fracture below the state:** Myanmar is the extreme case — the Kachin Independence Army, not the junta, now sets export taxes on the heavy rare earths (dysprosium/terbium) that feed China's magnet industry, while Laos's REE/potash boom runs on Chinese capital under heavy debt leverage with severe environmental blowback (Nam Sam River contamination; potash sinkholes). This is the same downstream-value-capture impulse seen in Indonesia/Vietnam, but with collapsed or contested state capacity.
- **EU as the regulatory keystone (the "Brussels Effect"):** the EU's binding Acts (AI Act, Chips Act, Critical Raw Materials Act) set the template that Germany, France, Netherlands, Italy, Spain, and Sweden all explicitly nest their national strategies inside (IPCEI, EuroHPC, Quantum Flagship). EU recovery funds (NextGenerationEU/PNRR, PERTEs, National Growth Fund) are the dominant financing engine across the European members.
- **Funding-model split:** US/Japan/Korea/Singapore/UK/Germany/France/Canada/Australia lean on public-private partnerships and targeted subsidies; China, India, Vietnam, and Indonesia use large state-directed missions and state-owned enterprises.
- **The nuclear fault line is the single sharpest divergence — and a global revival wave is unmistakable:**
  - *Aggressive build:* China, France, UK, South Korea, US (SMRs for AI/fab power).
  - *Revival/reversal:* Malaysia (RMK13), Indonesia (35 GW by 2060), Thailand (PDP 2024 SMRs), Vietnam (revived Ninh Thuan), Philippines (EO 164 + Bataan), Singapore (feasibility), India (Nuclear Energy Mission), Netherlands (NEO NL new-build), Italy (post-1987-referendum reversal), Sweden (~10 reactors by 2045), Spain (legislated phase-out now contested in parliament). The EU itself launched an SMR Alliance and added nuclear to its Taxonomy.
  - *Opt-out / banned:* Germany (full phase-out), Australia (legislative moratorium; nuclear only for AUKUS submarines), Israel (no civil program — strategic "ambiguity," non-NPT), **Ireland (statutory prohibition since 1999)**, and historically-non-nuclear Norway (now assessing its first SMR via the 2024 Nuclear Energy Commission + Aure/Heim impact assessment — no state construction funding).
  - *Already operating / building:* UAE (Barakah, 5,600 MW live), Saudi Arabia (SNAEP, with "optionality" on enrichment); Taiwan even reversed its completed phase-out to restart reactors. Turkey (Akkuyu, ~10% of power coming online), Poland (first Westinghouse plant ~2036), South Africa (extending Koeberg + 2,500 MW new build), Finland (Olkiluoto 3 live + Nuclear Act reform for SMRs), Brazil (completing Angra 3), **Egypt (El Dabaa, 4× VVER-1200 under construction with Rosatom), and Mexico (PRODESEN projects 2,350 MW of new nuclear)** all extend the build-out — making pro-nuclear momentum near-universal across the 46 entities. A distinctive *sovereign-to-commercial pivot* appears in **Argentina**, which is defunding its 80%-complete domestic SMR (CAREM) in favor of foreign commercial SMRs (ACR-300) to power AI data centers. **Kazakhstan**, the world's #1 uranium producer (Kazatomprom, ~43%), approved its first NPP by national referendum (71% yes, Oct 2024) and will pick an international consortium — moving up from raw-uranium exporter to power generator; **Ukraine** is replacing Russian-built capacity with nine Westinghouse AP1000s even under wartime strikes.
  - *Newly added bans/opt-outs:* **New Zealand** (the absolute Nuclear Free Zone Act 1987 — identity-defining), and **Oman** and **Qatar** (Gulf gas states that explicitly *opted out* of nuclear power, confining policy to radiation regulation while pursuing green hydrogen / renewables — diverging sharply from neighbors UAE and Saudi Arabia). **Chile** keeps "nucleoelectricity" research alive (CCHEN) but builds no plants given seismicity and renewables.
  - *Rosatom as the Global-South SMR vector:* Russia's Rosatom is the single most common partner for first-time nuclear entrants among smaller/developing states — anchoring **Laos** (2025–26 Roadmap, VVER/SMR), **Myanmar** (2023 SAC–Rosatom SMR agreement, 110→330 MWe), **Egypt** (El Dabaa), and **Turkey** (Akkuyu) — while **Brunei** (IAEA Milestones Approach) and **Cambodia** (Rosatom/CNNC MoUs + draft law) keep the option open via capacity-building rather than committed builds.
- **Resource leverage as industrial policy:** Indonesia (nickel *hilirisasi*), Vietnam (rare earths, 2nd-largest reserves), Malaysia (REE), Philippines (nickel/copper), Australia/Canada (critical-minerals strategies) all push downstream processing over raw export.
- **Demography as catalyst:** Japan, South Korea, Singapore, Germany, Australia, Malaysia, and Thailand explicitly justify AI/robotics/autonomy by labor shortages and aging.
- **Niche semiconductor plays:** UK, Canada (FABrIC), Australia, and the ASEAN states deliberately avoid the mass-fab subsidy race — specializing in design/IP, advanced packaging, compound semiconductors, photonics, and (for ASEAN) climbing from OSAT back-end toward front-end.
- **Quantum is overwhelmingly security-driven in emerging economies:** Indonesia's roadmap is run by its defense university; Malaysia, Vietnam, Thailand, and Philippines all foreground post-quantum cryptography over hardware supremacy.
- **Niche semiconductors extend into Europe too:** the Netherlands' Project Beethoven (€2.5B) is an ecosystem play to retain ASML rather than a fab subsidy; Sweden specializes in design/packaging/power electronics; Spain and Italy chase fabs (PERTE Chip €12.25B; Silicon Box in Novara) but hedge into design/RISC-V/photonics.
- **"Valley of death" commercialization is the shared European anxiety:** the Netherlands (Biotech Booster), Italy, Spain, and Sweden all explicitly fund mechanisms to convert strong public research into industry — a recurring self-diagnosis across the EU members.
- **Sovereign-wealth tech-building (the Gulf model):** UAE (MGX ~$100B; ADIA/Mubadala) and Saudi Arabia (PIF; Project Transcendence ~$100B; Alat $100B) deploy capital at a scale no R&D-grant system matches — buying into global compute/chips while building domestic full-stack ecosystems, Arabic LLMs (ALLaM/HUMAIN), and giga-project regulatory sandboxes (NEOM).
- **Taiwan + Israel = the chip/deep-tech anchors:** Taiwan's NT$300B CbI defends ~90% of advanced-logic production; Israel pivots "Startup Nation" software strength toward deep-tech (bio-convergence, quantum) — both leaning heavily on a military-intelligence talent pipeline and "build-to-last vs. build-to-exit" debates.
- **Switzerland's outsider strategy:** excluded from EU programs (Horizon/Digital Europe), it built sovereignty initiatives (SwissChips, Swiss Quantum Initiative) and prefers Council-of-Europe frameworks (signing the binding CoE AI Convention) over EU-style omnibus law.
- **Resource-leverage emerging economies:** Turkey (Beylikova rare earths, claimed 2nd-largest reserve), Brazil (niobium/rare earths + Strategic Minerals Policy), South Africa (PGMs/manganese beneficiation), and Finland (battery metals) all explicitly aim to capture downstream value and become non-China supply-chain nodes — the same playbook seen in Indonesia, Vietnam, Australia, and Saudi Arabia.
- **Defense-tech as a civilian on-ramp:** Turkey's globally dominant drone industry (Baykar) and TOGG, Finland's dual-use drone strategy (post-NATO accession), and Poland's early U-space leadership show smaller/mid powers using autonomous systems — often defense-driven — as their most competitive frontier. The most radical case is **Myanmar**, where civil war has made non-state actors (PDFs, EAOs) the global leader in *non-state* combat-drone use (82% of 3,000+ logged UAS incidents, 2020–2024) — autonomous systems advancing through conflict rather than policy.
- **Authoritarian digital control as its own "strategy":** in the foundational-tier and conflict states, digital policy can invert from enablement to control — **Myanmar's** Cybersecurity Law 1/2025 criminalizes VPNs, mandates 3-year data retention, and runs a ~1,500-site whitelist, while **Laos** (2024 Cybersecurity Law) and **Cambodia** pair connectivity goals with tightening data/cyber regimes. Sovereignty here means state visibility into the network, not just supply-chain independence.
- **Niche semiconductor specialization is now the global default:** outside the US/China/Taiwan/Korea/EU mega-fab race, virtually every other entity (UK, Canada, Australia, ASEAN, Switzerland, Finland, Norway, Poland, Ireland, South Africa, Brazil) targets back-end packaging, compound semiconductors, photonics, or design/IP rather than leading-edge logic fabs. Ireland is a partial exception — its 2025 *Silicon Island* strategy hosts 15 of the top 30 global chip firms and explicitly seeks a leading-edge fab.
- **Latin America's resource-nationalism vs. FDI-liberalization split:** Mexico and Argentina both anchor strategy on lithium but diverge sharply — **Mexico nationalized lithium** (LitioMx, a state monopoly barring private concessions) and pursues sovereign public tech (Nube MX cloud, Coatlicue supercomputer, the "Fábrica de IA"), whereas **Argentina** under Milei swung the opposite way, using **RIGI / Super RIGI** to offer 30-year fiscal stability and ICSID arbitration to attract FDI into lithium, SMRs, and chips while defunding sovereign programs. Both, plus Egypt (Egypt Makes Electronics, Kutsari-style design hubs), chase fabless/design and nearshoring rather than leading-edge fabs.
- **Securitized vs. liberalized drone regimes diverge most outside the OECD:** Egypt runs one of the world's most restrictive drone laws (Defense-controlled, military jurisdiction, recreational ban), while Argentina did the reverse in 2024 — abrogating restrictive VANT rules for an ICAO-aligned, risk-based RAAC framework legalizing BVLOS/autonomous flight. Norway, by contrast, leads in *maritime* autonomy (the Yara Birkeland autonomous container ship) and defense drones (post-Ukraine).
- **War is the most powerful accelerant of all — but only for battlefield-adjacent domains:** **Ukraine** is the defining case in the whole set. Under full-scale invasion it hyper-accelerated drones/defense-tech (Brave1 cluster, decentralized procurement, the gamified "Army of Drones" ePoints), e-governance (Diia — a wartime survival tool, now open-sourced), and microelectronics (security-driven), while basic science, biomedicine, and energy infrastructure were disrupted by brain drain and kinetic strikes. **Myanmar** shows the same asymmetry through civil war. War advances *applied* tech and stalls *foundational* tech.
- **The Gulf's two scarce strategic materials are not oil:** **Qatar** supplies ~30% of global **helium** (an irreplaceable input for chips, MRI, and quantum cooling — a 2026 Hormuz disruption spiked prices 40–100%), while **Oman** bets on **green hydrogen** (Hydrom: 1–1.5 Mt/yr by 2030) and green steel. Both, plus Qatar's IMEC R&D hub and Oman's ITHCA-funded "Oman-1/Oman-2" chips, show smaller Gulf states buying into the *semiconductor design* layer and sovereign Arabic LLMs (Fanar, Ma'een) rather than competing with the UAE/Saudi sovereign-wealth megafunds head-on.
- **The "new-oil" critical-minerals scramble spans every region by project's end:** beyond the earlier resource-leverage cases, **Kazakhstan** (calling REMs the "new oil," courted by both China and the EU/US), **Ukraine** (lithium/titanium/graphite as a reconstruction lifeline tied to EU/US funds), **Chile** (lithium via the state-led Codelco–SQM venture capturing 70–85% of operating margin), and **New Zealand** (a first-ever 2025 Minerals Strategy) all pivot toward downstream value capture and Western supply-chain alignment — the single most universal industrial-policy theme across all 46 entities.
- **Science-system architecture itself becomes the strategy:** **New Zealand** restructured its entire research base in 2025 (seven Crown Research Institutes → four Public Research Organisations, incl. a dedicated NZ Institute for Advanced Technology for AI/quantum) and ended its 20-year GMO moratorium (Gene Technology Bill) — showing that for smaller advanced economies, *consolidating and commercializing* the research system is as strategic as any single-domain plan. **Kazakhstan** similarly leads Central Asia on regulatory architecture (the 2026 Digital Code + a standalone AI Law).
- **Adoption-over-invention is the explicit small-economy doctrine:** New Zealand's first AI strategy ("Investing with Confidence") and Chile's data-center-led digital plan both *deliberately decline* to build foundational AI models, focusing capital on adoption, skills, and niche strengths (NZ photonics/space; Chile astronomy → data → compute). This mirrors the ASEAN foundational tier and contrasts with the sovereign-LLM push of the Gulf, China, and Korea.
