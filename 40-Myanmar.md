# Myanmar (Burma) — National Technology Strategies (8 Domains)

*Source: Gemini deep research, 2026. Entity 40 (Batch 8, final). ASEAN.*

Myanmar's technology governance cannot be read through a normal state-centric lens. Since the **February 2021 military coup**, the State Administration Council (SAC) issues formal policies, but actual control over critical tech sectors is contested by Ethnic Armed Organizations (EAOs) and People's Defence Forces (PDFs). Three patterns dominate: (1) **militarization** — AI and digital infrastructure reoriented toward surveillance, censorship, and security; (2) a **geopolitical pivot** to Russia (nuclear) and pragmatic China engagement (rare earths, hardware) under Western sanctions; and (3) **non-state governance** of the most valuable assets — the Kachin Independence Army now regulates the world-critical heavy-rare-earth supply chain, and non-state actors lead globally in battlefield drone use. Quantum and semiconductors are explicitly absent; biomedicine has collapsed.

## 1. Nuclear Energy
- **Plan:** Intergovernmental Agreement on Cooperation in the Use of Nuclear Energy; Roadmap on Cooperation in the Peaceful Use of Atomic Energy (2022–2023); Intergovernmental Agreement on Construction of a Small Modular Reactor (2025)
- **Body:** Ministry of Science and Technology (MoST) & Ministry of Electric Power; Russia's Rosatom & Rostekhnadzor
- **Year:** Roadmap Sept 2022; cooperation IGA Feb 2023; SMR construction IGA 4 Mar 2025
- **URL:** Global New Light of Myanmar (state media); Rosatom press releases; World Nuclear News
- **Summary:** Despite economic crisis, the SAC aggressively pursues nuclear tech, exclusively via Rosatom. The Feb 6, 2023 Intergovernmental Agreement (signed in Yangon before junta chief Min Aung Hlaing) targets: (1) a **Small Modular Reactor** using Russian pressurized-water tech — initial ≥110 MWe, expandable to 330 MWe (Rosatom did a 2022 land survey); (2) a regulatory framework via a MoST–Rostekhnadzor MOU; (3) human capital — Myanmar's first Nuclear Technology Information Centre opened Feb 2023 at Yangon University's Hlaing campus; and (4) a 15-member implementation committee chaired by Min Aung Hlaing (Defense Minister as vice-chair). On **March 4, 2025**, during Min Aung Hlaing's state visit to Moscow, the two governments signed a more concrete Intergovernmental Agreement on the **construction** of an onshore SMR using Rosatom's RITM reactor technology — a 110 MWe unit expandable to 330 MWe — explicitly framed as a remedy for Myanmar's severe power shortages and a cement of Russia's energy footprint in Southeast Asia. Official texts span 14 application areas (agriculture, health, electricity), but financing remains opaque — observers doubt the cash-strapped, sanctioned regime can fund a multi-billion-dollar SMR.

## 2. Artificial Intelligence
- **Plan:** Draft National AI Strategy (2025–2030) & Draft National AI Policy
- **Body:** Ministry of Science and Technology (National AI Strategy & Policy Drafting Committee)
- **Year:** 2025 (late-stage drafting)
- **URL:** Ministry of Information; Myanmar National Portal
- **Summary:** A dual reality — a formal drafting effort alongside practical AI weaponization. The MoST held its 4th coordination meeting (Feb 2025, Nay Pyi Taw) to finalize the draft Strategy/Policy, which draws on the ASEAN Guide on AI Governance and Ethics (fairness, accountability, privacy, transparency), emphasizes workforce development and "Offline-First" applications (given energy/connectivity gaps), and pairs with a Myanmar e-Governance Master Plan 2030 (Burmese-language GenAI chatbots, a Social Protection Integrity Engine). In stark contrast, human-rights researchers report the SAC has built AI into a surveillance ecosystem — facial recognition to track dissidents and keyword scanning of intercepted communications for terms like "protest"/"revolution." Civilian AI funding is constrained while resources flow to AI-powered repression.

## 3. Quantum Information Science
- **Plan:** *Explicitly absent* — no strategy, funding, or roadmap
- **Body:** N/A
- **Year:** N/A
- **URL:** N/A
- **Summary:** Quantum information science, computing, and sensing are entirely absent from Myanmar's strategic framework. The country lacks the institutional capacity, research infrastructure, and capital to engage. While Myanmar appears on administrative lists in some global quantum consortiums, there are no state-sponsored quantum programs, funding allocations, or policy roadmaps.

## 4. Biomedical Sciences / Biotechnology
- **Plan:** Guideline for Submission to Ethics Review Committee; Guidelines on Clinical Trials of Traditional Drugs (legacy)
- **Body:** Department of Medical Research (DMR), Ministry of Health
- **Year:** 2016 (ethics guidelines); pre-coup health plans — now stalled
- **URL:** Ministry of Health; US HHS Compilation of Human Research Standards
- **Summary:** Pre-2021, the DMR oversaw biomedical strategy weighted toward infectious-disease control (malaria, TB, HIV), traditional-medicine modernization, and BSL-3 lab capacity (post-BTWC ratification). Notable advances: 2016–2018 whole-genome sequencing and phylogenetic tracking of MDR-TB in Yangon, plus efforts to expand border bio-surveillance and dual-use research regulation. Post-coup, these goals are entirely stalled — the coup decimated healthcare/education, fractured the workforce (mass walkouts), and severed international funding and academic collaborations. Biomedical policy is in indefinite suspension, prioritizing emergency trauma care over advanced R&D.

## 5. Materials Science / Critical Raw Materials (Rare Earths)
- **Plan:** *De facto* KIA taxation and export regulatory directives (no enforceable unified state law in the region)
- **Body:** Kachin Independence Army (KIA) / Kachin Independence Organization (KIO)
- **Year:** Late 2024 / early 2025 (new tariff structures)
- **URL:** ISP-Myanmar, Adamas Intelligence, on-the-ground reporting
- **Summary:** The starkest case of state failure and non-state tech governance. Myanmar is the world's 3rd-largest rare-earth producer and China's primary external source — exporting 290,000+ tons worth ~$4.2B (2017–2024), 85% extracted post-coup — from heavy-REE ion-adsorption clays in the Chipwi-Pangwa belt of Kachin State (dysprosium, terbium). In October 2024 the KIA seized Chipwi and Pangwa, becoming a proto-state governing a critical global supply chain. After Kunming negotiations, the KIA set a *de facto* framework in early 2025: a fixed export tax of 35,000 CNY (~$4,830) per metric ton of concentrate plus a 20% volumetric levy — potentially >$200M/yr at historical ~31,000-ton volumes, funding KIA operations independent of the SAC. The toxic in-situ leaching (2,500+ pits in Chipwi alone) has caused severe groundwater contamination, sparking local Kachin protests against the KIA's own mining policies.

## 6. Semiconductors / Microelectronics
- **Plan:** *Explicitly absent* — no strategy, funding, or infrastructure
- **Body:** N/A
- **Year:** N/A
- **URL:** N/A
- **Summary:** Semiconductors/microelectronics are entirely absent from Myanmar's strategies. While Malaysia, Thailand, and Vietnam build assembly/testing/packaging and fab hubs, Myanmar is excluded. Barriers: a human-capital deficit (one engineer per 5,000–7,000 citizens vs. ~1 per 2,000 in Malaysia/Singapore) and geopolitical instability — even India has avoided tech ventures given border insurgencies and political risk. The SAC has no published semiconductor policy, funding, or projects.

## 7. Digital Technology / Digital Infrastructure
- **Plan:** Cybersecurity Law No. 1/2025 (in force 30 Jul 2025 via Notification No. 113/2025); Myanmar Digital Economy Roadmap 2030; Myanmar e-Governance Master Plan 2030 (draft, legacy)
- **Body:** State Administration Council (SAC) → State Security and Prosperity Commission (SSPC, from Aug 2025); National-Level Digital Economy Development Committee (DEDC); Ministry of Transport and Communications (MOTC)
- **Year:** Cybersecurity Law enacted 1 Jan 2025, in force 30 Jul 2025; Digital Economy Roadmap 2030 advanced 2025
- **URL:** State-run newspapers; MOTC; SAC/SSPC Office (sacoffice.gov.mm)
- **Summary:** Under civilian rule, digital strategy aimed at e-governance and integration (the Digital Economy Roadmap 2018–2025 and e-Government Master Plan targeted 80% internet penetration, 68,000 km of fiber). Post-coup these are eclipsed by the **Cybersecurity Law No. 1/2025** (enacted Jan 1, 2025; officially **entering into force July 30, 2025** via SAC Notification No. 113/2025) to protect "state sovereignty" and suppress opposition: it criminalizes unauthorized VPNs (up to 6 months prison, 5M MMK ~$2,700 fine), requires digital platforms with over 100,000 users to obtain operating licenses, mandates ISPs/platforms retain user data (names, IP logs) for up to 3 years, asserts extraterritorial jurisdiction over Myanmar citizens abroad, and underpins rolling internet blackouts (Sagaing, Rakhine) and a "whitelist" model permitting only ~1,500 state-approved sites while blocking Facebook/Twitter. In mid-2025 the regime's DEDC advanced a successor **Myanmar Digital Economy Roadmap 2030** (1 vision, 6 objectives, 9 priority sectors, 77 action plans) that, in practice, prioritizes digital IDs, biometrics, and the nationwide "Person Scrutinization and Monitoring System" (PSMS) built largely on imported Chinese surveillance tech. Governance also shifted: on **August 1, 2025** the military dissolved the SAC and replaced it with the **State Security and Prosperity Commission (SSPC)**, which oversaw staged elections held December 2025–January 2026. The net result is a closed, intranet-style ecosystem that has decimated online commerce and the digital economy.

## 8. Autonomous Systems
- **Plan:** Advisory Circular DCA-AC-AIR-01(R1): Restriction of Drone Operations *(statutory framework bypassed by civil war)*
- **Body:** Department of Civil Aviation (DCA), Ministry of Transport and Communications
- **Year:** December 2019
- **URL:** https://www.dcamyanmar.com
- **Summary:** Two spheres — outdated civilian aviation rules on paper, and decentralized drone warfare on the battlefield. The official circular (2019) requires DCA written permission for commercial/specialized flights, bars flying within 8 km of aerodromes or near government/military areas, and restricts drone imports (illegal import up to 3 years prison). These are irrelevant in conflict zones: anti-junta PDFs and EAOs (e.g., the Three Brotherhood Alliance, Operation 1027) industrialized UAS use. A UNIDIR report logged 3,041 UAS incidents (2020–2024), 82% (2,504) by non-state armed groups — making Myanmar the global leader for non-state UAS deployment. Rebels use modified commercial quadcopters for ISR/munition-dropping and craft-produced fixed-wing "kamikaze" drones reaching Naypyitaw. The Tatmadaw has responded by procuring drone jammers and fielding its own kamikaze/ISR drones. No robotics R&D strategy exists.
