# New Zealand (Aotearoa) — National Technology Strategies (8 Domains)

*Source: Gemini deep research, 2026. Entity 46 (Batch 9, final). OECD / Pacific.*

New Zealand's 2024–2025 policy landscape is defined by its most profound science-system restructuring in 30+ years: under the Gluckman/MBIE reforms (effective July 2025), Callaghan Innovation was disestablished and seven Crown Research Institutes merged into four **Public Research Organisations** — including the new **NZ Institute for Advanced Technology (NZIAT)** for AI and quantum. The 2025 agenda is aggressively modernizing: a *first-ever* National AI Strategy (adoption over foundational development), ending the two-decade GMO moratorium (Gene Technology Bill), and a new Critical Minerals Strategy. Two distinctive identity markers: the absolute **Nuclear Free Zone Act 1987** and a world-class space sector (Rocket Lab, NZ Space Agency). Semiconductors and quantum remain niche/nascent.

## 1. Nuclear Energy
- **Plan:** New Zealand Nuclear Free Zone, Disarmament, and Arms Control Act 1987 *(legal prohibition; no nuclear power)*
- **Body:** Parliament of New Zealand (administered by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs and Trade)
- **Year:** 1987 (ongoing enforcement)
- **URL:** https://www.legislation.govt.nz/act/public/1987/86/en/latest/
- **Summary:** New Zealand's nuclear-free stance is embedded in national identity, foreign policy, and law. The 1987 Act establishes a Nuclear Free Zone (land, airspace, territorial waters to 12 nm), absolutely banning nuclear weapons and nuclear-powered vessels, and domesticating the NPT and the Treaty of Rarotonga (South Pacific Nuclear Free Zone). Notably, the Act does *not* strictly prohibit land-based nuclear power plants or medical/research isotopes — but as sustained policy and economic reality, New Zealand has zero nuclear plants and no nuclear-energy strategy (medical/research isotopes are permitted under health regulations). Governance runs through PACDAC; the PM alone approves foreign-warship entry. The stance historically led to the suspension of US ANZUS obligations.

## 2. Artificial Intelligence
- **Plan:** New Zealand's Strategy for Artificial Intelligence: "Investing with Confidence"
- **Body:** Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment (MBIE)
- **Year:** July 2025
- **URL:** https://www.mbie.govt.nz/business-and-employment/economic-growth/digital-policy/new-zealands-ai-strategy-investing-with-confidence
- **Summary:** New Zealand's first cohesive national AI strategy (it had been the last OECD member without one). It is pragmatic and adoption-focused — explicitly *not* attempting native foundational models (infeasible at NZ's scale) but driving rapid, responsible commercial uptake. Macro projections: AI could add NZD $76–108B to GDP by 2038 (generative AI alone >15% of GDP). The regulatory posture is "light-touch," principles-based — industry self-regulation plus existing law (Privacy Act 2020), aligned with OECD AI Principles, supplemented by voluntary *Responsible AI Guidance for Businesses*. Funding targets adoption/skills, not compute: a $17M Responsible AI Adopt Programme for SMEs, $213M (Budget 2025) for workforce training, $64M for STEM, and 15% R&D tax credits. Advanced AI research sits in the new NZIAT.

## 3. Quantum Information Science
- **Plan:** Quantum Technologies Aotearoa (Catalyst Strategic) + NZIAT Quantum Discovery Phase
- **Body:** MBIE / NZ Institute for Advanced Technology (NZIAT) / Dodd-Walls Centre
- **Year:** 2023 (Catalyst fund); December 2025 (NZIAT phase)
- **URL:** https://www.mbie.govt.nz/about/news/new-zealand-institute-for-advanced-technology-discovery-phase-for-quantum-technologies
- **Summary:** No finalized standalone national quantum strategy, but quantum has rapidly formalized. Two mechanisms drive it: (1) **Quantum Technologies Aotearoa** (2023, NZD $12M 5-year Catalyst Strategic investment), led by the Te Whai Ao – Dodd-Walls Centre for Photonic and Quantum Technologies (micro-resonators, optical microcombs, cold atoms); and (2) the NZIAT's December 2025 six-month "discovery phase" (NZD $1.35M) to map capabilities and design a permanent national quantum platform. The approach is cooperative and niche — photonics, quantum memory/sensing, post-quantum cryptography, and optimization (Ising machines) — leveraging a >NZD $1.2B photonics sector growing ~10%/yr, with international partnerships (UK, US, Germany, Japan, Singapore, Australia; the NZ-UK FTA explicitly facilitates quantum/photonics collaboration).

## 4. Biomedical Sciences / Biotechnology
- **Plan:** Gene Technology Bill 2024 (ends the GMO moratorium)
- **Body:** Parliament / Environmental Protection Authority (EPA) / MBIE
- **Year:** Introduced late 2024; enactment target late 2025
- **URL:** https://www.mbie.govt.nz/science-and-technology/science-and-innovation/agencies-policies-and-budget-initiatives/gene-technology-regulation
- **Summary:** A historic statutory shift. For 20+ years New Zealand had one of the world's strictest anti-GMO regimes (under the HSNO Act 1996). The Gene Technology Bill ends this de facto moratorium, aligning with global standards (notably Australia). It separates gene technology from the hazardous-substances regime, establishes an independent Gene Technology Regulator within the EPA (with Technical and Māori Advisory Committees), and creates a risk-tiered system — minimal-risk edits (SDN-1, mimicking conventional breeding) are Category 1 and effectively exempt from heavy GMO regulation/labeling, while Category 2+ remain regulated as GMOs. It also removes local-authority power to restrict gene tech (unified national standard). Research concentrates in two new PROs — NZ Institute for Bioeconomy Science (NZIBS) and NZ Institute for Public Health and Forensic Science. The reform is contentious: proponents (Life Sciences Network) cite agricultural-export competitiveness; opponents (GE Free NZ, organic farmers, Māori advocates) cite the premium "GE-Free" brand and Treaty of Waitangi / Wai 262 kaitiakitanga obligations.

## 5. Materials Science / Critical Raw Materials
- **Plan:** A Minerals Strategy for New Zealand to 2040 + Critical Minerals List
- **Body:** MBIE / New Zealand Petroleum & Minerals
- **Year:** January 2025
- **URL:** https://www.beehive.govt.nz/sites/default/files/2025-01/202501%20A%20Minerals%20Strategy%20for%20New%20Zealand%20to%202040.pdf
- **Summary:** A profound posture shift in January 2025 — an export-driven roadmap to turn geology into economic muscle amid the clean-energy transition and resource security. Central target: double mineral exports from ~NZD $1.46B (2023) to **NZD $3B by 2035**, creating 7,000 regional jobs. While gold/coal/aggregates remain foundational short-term, the strategy pivots toward critical minerals for batteries/solar/wind (rare earths, antimony, calcined clays for lower-carbon concrete). Three pillars: enhancing prosperity (domestic processing, streamlined permits, FDI), demonstrating value (resource data, communities, Te Tiriti obligations), and delivering for the clean-energy transition (supply-chain resilience). It preserves environmental protection by confirming "Schedule 4 Land" (Crown Minerals Act 1991) stays off-limits to mining; research is augmented by the new NZ Institute for Earth Science (NZIES).

## 6. Semiconductors / Microelectronics
- **Plan:** *Nascent / absent* — no national semiconductor strategy
- **Body:** Private sector (e.g., Rakon); indirect MBIE support; future R&D under NZIAT
- **Year:** N/A
- **URL:** N/A (sector advocacy via https://technewzealand.org.nz)
- **Summary:** Unlike the US (CHIPS Act) or EU, New Zealand has no dedicated semiconductor/microelectronics strategy — foundry capital intensity is unsuited to its scale. The domain is niche and private-sector-led, concentrated in photonics and frequency-control/timing. The standout is **Rakon** (Auckland) — a global leader in high-precision microchips for 5G, aerospace, LEO satellites, and AI hardware (launched its *Niku* AI computing semiconductor in late 2023, then the *MercuryX* platform, expanding its addressable market ~10% to ~$3.7B). Reliant on private capital, such firms are acquisition-prone: in early 2026 Rakon received a NZD $356M takeover offer from US-based Bourns Inc. Advocacy groups (TechNZ) urge MBIE to fund early-stage deep-tech to retain IP domestically; future microelectronics R&D is slated to fall under NZIAT.

## 7. Digital Technology / Digital Infrastructure
- **Plan:** The Digital Strategy for Aotearoa (+ Action Plan) + New Zealand Space and Advanced Aviation Strategy 2024–2030
- **Body:** MBIE, Department of Internal Affairs (DIA), Stats NZ, NZ Space Agency
- **Year:** 2022 (Digital Strategy); September 2024 (Space Strategy)
- **URL:** https://www.digital.govt.nz/digital-government/strategy/digital-strategy-for-aotearoa-and-action-plan / https://www.mbie.govt.nz/assets/new-zealand-space-and-advanced-aviation-strategy-2024-2030.pdf
- **Summary:** A mature pillar across terrestrial digital equity and orbital expansion. The **Digital Strategy for Aotearoa** targets a thriving digital economy by 2032 on three themes: Mahi Tika (trust — cybersecurity, ethical data governance, Digital Identity Services Trust Framework, Te Ao Māori data sovereignty), Mahi Tahi (inclusion — UFB/Rural Broadband, Marae Digital Capability), and Mahi Ake (growth — Digital Boost). In parallel, New Zealand's **space sector** (governed by the Outer Space and High-altitude Activities Act 2017, leveraging Rocket Lab) targets doubling aerospace value to **NZD $10B/yr by 2030**. Led by the NZ Space Agency, the policy emphasizes sovereign space capabilities, debris removal, and sustainable orbital access — including a NZD $29.35M state investment in MethaneSAT (launched 2024), NZ's first government-funded space mission.

## 8. Autonomous Systems
- **Plan:** Civil Aviation Rules Part 101/102 refresh + "Transport Instruments"
- **Body:** Civil Aviation Authority of New Zealand (CAA) / Ministry of Transport
- **Year:** December 2025 (rules enactment; under the Civil Aviation Act 2023)
- **URL:** https://www.aviation.govt.nz/drones/regulations/part-101-rules-for-drones/
- **Summary:** New Zealand is rapidly adapting to integrate drones/UAS. Historically a "separation-first" model under Part 101 (lower risk) and Part 102 (higher-risk/commercial), requiring VLOS and daylight-only ops. The December 2025 amendments introduced agile **Transport Instruments** (e.g., CATI: 101-1) — secondary legislation letting the Director of Civil Aviation update thresholds as tech evolves. Operators can now do BVLOS and night flights in controlled/enclosed environments without burdensome Part 102 certification, cutting R&D friction — but autonomous agritech (aerial spraying/topdressing) was explicitly moved to Part 102 due to its risk profile. Military integration is driven by the NZ Army's Robotics and Autonomous Systems (RAS) Strategy — Manned-Unmanned Teaming, small UAS, Remote Ground Sensors, and 2024 contracts for Micro UAS swarms (deployable <75 seconds). The Space & Advanced Aviation Strategy brands NZ as a flexible testing ground for next-gen automated flight.
