# United States — National Technology Strategies (8 Domains)

*Source: Gemini deep research, 2026. Country 1 of 5 (Batch 1). OECD bloc.*

The U.S. uses a decentralized, "whole-of-government" model coordinated by the White House Office of Science and Technology Policy (OSTP) and the National Science and Technology Council (NSTC), rather than a single technology ministry. Geopolitical competition (supply-chain resilience, national security) is the dominant driver, and most plans favor public-private partnerships. Since 2025 the second Trump administration has shifted the posture toward an aggressive, executive-order-driven, deregulatory and security-first model — a wave of presidential actions (the July 2025 "America's AI Action Plan," the May 2025 nuclear executive orders, the November 2025 "Genesis Mission," a June 2026 post-quantum cryptography order, and Section 232 trade proclamations on critical minerals and semiconductors) has largely superseded the prior strategic-plan documents as the operative national strategy.

## 1. Nuclear Energy
- **Plan:** Executive Order 14302 "Reinvigorating the Nuclear Industrial Base" (issued alongside EO 14299 "Deploying Advanced Nuclear Reactor Technologies for National Security" and EO 14300 "Ordering the Reform of the Nuclear Regulatory Commission")
- **Agency:** The White House (implemented by DOE / NRC); supersedes the operative posture of the 2021 DOE Office of Nuclear Energy Strategic Vision
- **Year:** 2025 (May 23, 2025)
- **URL:** https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/05/29/2025-09801/reinvigorating-the-nuclear-industrial-base
- **Summary:** A package of four executive orders signed May 23, 2025 marks a decisive acceleration of U.S. nuclear policy. EO 14302 directs a domestic nuclear fuel bank, expanded HALEU and enrichment capacity, and a quadrupling of U.S. nuclear generation by 2050; EO 14299 fast-tracks advanced reactor deployment (including reactors to power AI data centers and defense installations); and EO 14300 orders a sweeping reform of the Nuclear Regulatory Commission with fixed licensing deadlines. The actions retain the prior Strategic Vision's emphasis on SMRs, microreactors, accident-tolerant fuels and secure domestic HALEU supply, but reframe them as urgent national-security and reindustrialization priorities. National labs and public-private partnerships remain the primary delivery mechanism.

## 2. Artificial Intelligence
- **Plan:** America's AI Action Plan ("Winning the AI Race"), implemented through accompanying executive orders; reinforced by EO 14365 "Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence" (Dec 2025)
- **Agency:** The White House / OSTP (supersedes the 2023 National AI R&D Strategic Plan as the operative national strategy)
- **Year:** 2025 (released July 23, 2025)
- **URL:** https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/Americas-AI-Action-Plan.pdf
- **Summary:** A deregulation-first national plan organized around three pillars — accelerating AI innovation, building American AI infrastructure (data centers, energy, chips), and leading in international AI diplomacy and security/export of the "American AI stack." It rolls back the prior administration's risk-and-safety framing (rescinding EO 14110) and was issued with companion executive orders on data-center permitting, AI exports, and "preventing woke AI" in federal procurement. EO 14365 (Dec 11, 2025) further seeks a uniform national policy framework that pre-empts a fragmented patchwork of state AI laws. The earlier National AI R&D Strategic Plan (2023) and NIST evaluation work remain relevant on the research/standards side, but national posture has shifted sharply toward speed, scale and competitiveness with China.

## 3. Quantum Information Science
- **Plan:** Executive Order 14413 "Ushering in the Next Frontier of Quantum Innovation" (building on the 2018 National Strategic Overview for QIS and the National Quantum Initiative Act)
- **Agency:** The White House (with NIST, OMB, NSTC Subcommittee on QIS)
- **Year:** 2026 (June 22, 2026)
- **URL:** https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/06/ushering-in-the-next-frontier-of-quantum-innovation/
- **Summary:** This 2026 executive order elevates quantum to a top-tier national-security and competitiveness priority, directing accelerated federal investment in quantum computing, sensing and networking and hardening U.S. systems against "harvest-now, decrypt-later" cryptographic threats. It is paired with an OMB memorandum on executing the federal migration to post-quantum cryptography (NIST PQC standards), which sets hard transition deadlines of December 2030 for key establishment and December 2031 for digital signatures across agencies and contractors. The foundational 2018 National Strategic Overview and the $1.2B National Quantum Initiative still anchor the "science-first" research base, multidisciplinary centers, and "quantum-smart" workforce. The new posture stresses execution, supply-chain security, and allied standards-setting.

## 4. Biomedical Sciences / Biotechnology
- **Plan:** Charting the Future of Biotechnology: An Action Plan for American Security and Prosperity (NSCEB final report to Congress); succeeds the 2023 "Bold Goals" framework
- **Agency:** National Security Commission on Emerging Biotechnology (NSCEB), congressionally chartered
- **Year:** 2025 (final report delivered April 2025)
- **URL:** https://www.biotech.senate.gov/final-report
- **Summary:** The NSCEB's April 2025 final report recasts biotechnology as a strategic-competition priority versus China, with 49 recommendations across six pillars. Headline proposals include standing up a National Biotechnology Coordination Office in the Executive Office of the President, treating biological data as a critical strategic resource (with defensive measures against adversary access), creating expedited regulatory pathways to scale U.S. bioproducts, funding "grand challenge" research for leap-ahead capabilities, and building regional biomanufacturing hubs and a skilled workforce. It largely supersedes the Biden-era "Bold Goals for U.S. Biotechnology and Biomanufacturing" (2023) as the operative national biotech strategy, shifting emphasis from climate/societal goals toward security, the bioeconomy, and out-innovating competitors. The Genesis Mission (Nov 2025) further channels DOE supercomputing and AI foundation models into biotech grand challenges.

## 5. Materials Science
- **Plan:** Materials Genome Initiative Strategic Plan
- **Agency:** NSTC
- **Year:** 2021
- **URL:** https://www.mgi.gov/sites/mgi/files/MGI-2021-Strategic-Plan.pdf
- **Summary:** The Materials Genome Initiative remains the standing R&D framework — accelerating discovery, manufacturing, and deployment of advanced materials at lower cost via a unified "Materials Innovation Infrastructure" integrating computational modeling, autonomous/high-throughput experimentation, and large-scale AI/ML data. It is now complemented by sharp critical-minerals trade action: Section 232 Proclamation 11001 (Jan 14, 2026, "Adjusting Imports of Processed Critical Minerals and Their Derivative Products") frames import dependence on processed critical minerals as a national-security threat and pairs negotiation windows with tariff authority to onshore processing capacity. The 2025 nuclear and AI infrastructure push (HALEU, magnets, battery and semiconductor materials) further raises the strategic stakes for domestic materials supply chains. Workforce expansion for defense, clean energy, and semiconductors continues.

## 6. Semiconductors / Microelectronics
- **Plan:** National Strategy on Microelectronics Research (Amended)
- **Agency:** NSTC Subcommittee on Microelectronics Leadership / OSTP
- **Year:** 2023 (amended/re-released 2025); trade action 2026
- **URL:** https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Amended-National-Strategy-on-Microelectronics-Research.pdf
- **Summary:** The Amended National Strategy on Microelectronics Research remains the R&D backbone — maximizing CHIPS and Science Act investments through a five-year framework on advanced packaging, novel circuit designs, post-quantum hardware security, and closing the "lab-to-fab" gap. It is now reinforced by Section 232 Proclamation 11002 (Jan 14, 2026, "Adjusting Imports of Semiconductors, Semiconductor Manufacturing Equipment, and Their Derivative Products"), which imposes tariffs (reported ~25%) on imported advanced logic chips and equipment lacking domestic build-out commitments, tying market access to U.S. fab investment. Together with the AI Action Plan's chip-infrastructure pillar, policy now couples R&D, industrial subsidies, and trade leverage to force-onshore manufacturing. Supply-chain resilience and a domestic microelectronics workforce remain core goals.

## 7. Digital Technology / Digital Infrastructure
- **Plan:** U.S. International Cyberspace and Digital Policy Strategy
- **Agency:** U.S. Department of State
- **Year:** 2024 (international strategy); 2025 domestic-infrastructure EOs
- **URL:** https://2021-2025.state.gov/united-states-international-cyberspace-and-digital-policy-strategy/
- **Summary:** The 2024 State Department strategy (now archived) introduced "digital solidarity" — aligning democratic allies around an open, secure, rights-respecting digital ecosystem across four areas: resilient digital infrastructure, ethical data governance, responsible state behavior in cyberspace, and partner cyber capacity-building. Under the 2025 administration the operative emphasis has shifted to domestic digital infrastructure tied to the AI buildout: a July 23, 2025 executive order "Accelerating Federal Permitting of Data Center Infrastructure" (issued with the AI Action Plan) fast-tracks data centers, power, and supporting networks, treating compute and energy capacity as strategic infrastructure. The international agenda has pivoted from rights-and-governance framing toward exporting the "American AI technology stack" and countering Chinese telecom/standards influence. Telecom supply-chain security remains a throughline.

## 8. Autonomous Systems
- **Plan:** Executive Order 14307 "Unleashing American Drone Dominance" (June 6, 2025), implemented via the DoD memorandum "Unleashing U.S. Military Drone Dominance" (July 10, 2025) and OMB Memo M-26-02 on the American Secure Drone Act (Dec 2025)
- **Agency:** The White House / Department of Defense / OMB (legacy framework: DoD Unmanned Systems Integrated Roadmap)
- **Year:** 2025
- **URL:** https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/06/11/2025-12966/unleashing-american-drone-dominance
- **Note:** The U.S. still lacks a unified *civilian* national robotics/autonomy strategy; policy remains defense-led but is now far more activist. The new actions explicitly target scaling low-cost, AI-enabled, attritable drones and securing the drone supply chain against Chinese components.
- **Summary:** EO 14307 directs federal agencies to accelerate domestic drone manufacturing, expand BVLOS (beyond-visual-line-of-sight) operations, prioritize U.S.-made systems in procurement, and counter unauthorized/foreign drones. The accompanying DoD memo orders the services to field thousands of low-cost autonomous systems rapidly, cuts acquisition red tape, and pushes "manned-unmanned teaming" and software-defined platforms. OMB Memo M-26-02 implements the American Secure Drone Act, restricting federal use of drones with covered (Chinese) components and hardening the supply chain. The decades-old Unmanned Systems Integrated Roadmap (FY2013-2038) and branch strategies remain background, but operational urgency and resourcing have sharply increased.
