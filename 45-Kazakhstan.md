# Kazakhstan — National Technology Strategies (8 Domains)

*Source: Gemini deep research, 2026. Entity 45 (Batch 9). Central Asia.*

Kazakhstan leverages a "multi-vector" foreign policy to position itself as a technology-supply-chain node balancing China, Russia, the EU, and the US — and is pivoting from raw extraction toward deep processing and a high-value digital economy. It is setting Central Asian regulatory precedents with the 2026 **Digital Code** and a landmark **Law on Artificial Intelligence**. Maturity varies sharply: digital infrastructure, critical minerals, and nuclear energy are mature; quantum, semiconductors, and autonomous systems remain nascent (academic/JV-driven). Defining assets: world #1 uranium producer (Kazatomprom, ~43%), a fresh 2024 referendum approving its first NPP, and ~130 rare-earth deposits courted by both East and West.

## 1. Nuclear Energy
- **Plan:** Decree on the Republican Referendum on NPP Construction (approved Oct 2024) + selection of an international consortium
- **Body:** President of Kazakhstan (Tokayev) / Central Referendum Commission; Kazatomprom (fuel)
- **Year:** 2024
- **URL:** https://www.akorda.kz / https://www.election.gov.kz
- **Summary:** As the world's largest uranium producer (~43% of global output in 2022 via Kazatomprom), Kazakhstan is moving up the nuclear value chain. Despite a painful legacy (450+ Soviet tests at Semipalatinsk; the BN-350 fast breeder decommissioned in 1999, leaving two decades without nuclear power), worsening electricity deficits from aging coal plants drove an October 6, 2024 national referendum — **71.12% approved** building the first NPP (63.66% turnout). The plant is slated for Ülken near Lake Balkhash (amid ecological debate). Per the multi-vector policy, Tokayev favors an "international consortium," with contenders Rosatom (Russia), CNNC (China, BOT model), KHNP (South Korea), and EDF (France). A Russia SMR JV was terminated over IP disputes; the focus is a large-scale consortium NPP to offset the power deficit and localize the civil nuclear industry.

## 2. Artificial Intelligence
- **Plan:** Concept for the Development of Artificial Intelligence 2024–2029 (Decree No. 592) + Law on Artificial Intelligence
- **Body:** Government (Ministry of Digital Development, Innovations and Aerospace Industry, MDDIAI) / Parliament
- **Year:** 2024 (Concept); 2025/2026 (Law)
- **URL:** https://adilet.zan.kz/rus/docs/P2400000592 / https://www.akorda.kz
- **Summary:** The Concept (July 2024) — overseen by the new AI and Innovation Development Committee — candidly notes Kazakhstan ranked 72nd on the Government AI Readiness Index (2023). Quantitative targets: 5× more AI-integrated products by 2029, training up to 5 million citizens, and ~$20B injected into the economy by 2030 via AI automation in oil/gas, mining, energy, transport, agriculture. Infrastructure sovereignty centers on **KazLLM** (a sovereign Kazakh-language model, trained with Nazarbayev University), a national supercomputer, a National AI Platform, and the **Alem.AI** international hub. The **Law on AI** (enacted late 2025, in force early 2026) makes Kazakhstan a Central Asian regulatory pioneer: AI systems are "objects of informatization" (tools under human control), with risk-based classification, mandatory deepfake/synthetic-content labeling (machine-readable + visual), and administrative liability up to suspension/banning of non-compliant systems.

## 3. Quantum Information Science
- **Plan:** Kazakh-Singaporean Center for Quantum Technologies *(no standalone national quantum strategy)*
- **Body:** Ministry of Science and Higher Education / Al-Farabi Kazakh National University (KazNU)
- **Year:** 2026 (launch)
- **URL:** https://farabi.university
- **Summary:** Explicitly nascent — no overarching national quantum strategy; the domain is incubated in academia. The milestone is the February 2026 inauguration of the **Kazakh-Singaporean Center for Quantum Technologies** at KazNU (Almaty) — a trilateral collaboration of KazNU, Singapore's ASTRASEC, and domestic startup Qubitera. Priorities: quantum cybersecurity/cryptography (a first-phase lab with photonic systems for quantum-secured communications), a talent pipeline bridging academic physics and industry, and alignment with UN SDGs 4 and 17. The international tech-transfer approach signals intent to avoid falling behind in the post-silicon "quantum revolution."

## 4. Biomedical Sciences / Biotechnology
- **Plan:** Strategic programs of QazBioPharm & the National Center for Biotechnology (NCB)
- **Body:** Ministry of Healthcare / QazBioPharm JSC
- **Year:** NCB established 1993; continuous updates
- **URL:** http://www.biocenter.kz / https://qbp-holding.kz
- **Summary:** The NCB (1993, presidential decree) runs 22 specialized labs (biosafety, pharmacology, cell tech, applied genetics) as a subsidiary of state holding **QazBioPharm**, which coordinates biosecurity and domestic biomanufacturing. Kazakhstan developed its own COVID-19 vaccine (**QazVac**), proving end-to-end pharmaceutical capability. A recent leap: QazBioPharm partnered with China's **BGI Genomics** to launch the **Astana Genetic Center** — Central Asia's first such lab — ending the need to export biomaterials for sequencing, spanning precision-medicine biomarkers and agricultural ecology (anti-desertification genomics near the Aral Sea). On agri-biotech, policy is strictly conservative: GE-crop commercialization/cultivation is banned (limited to labs), and Kazakhstan defers to the EAEU (effectively Russian) framework for GE imports — blocking commercialization of domestically engineered varieties.

## 5. Materials Science / Critical Raw Materials
- **Plan:** Critical-minerals deep-processing directives (via National Geological Service)
- **Body:** Ministry of Industry and Construction / National Geological Service
- **Year:** Service est. 2022; updates 2024–2025
- **URL:** https://www.gov.kz
- **Summary:** Kazakhstan produces 19 of the 34 materials the EU deems vital (titanium, copper, magnesium, scandium) and holds ~130 rare-earth/associated-mineral deposits (lithium, beryllium, tantalum). Currently ~100% of REM output (2023–24) went to China, but as the US/EU decouple from Beijing, Astana is positioning as a multi-vector processing hub. The early-2025 "Zhana Kazakhstan" discovery (Karagandy, ~20M tonnes of REM-bearing ore) could make it the world's #3 in REM reserves. Tokayev calls REMs the "new oil," prioritizing four processing ventures: battery materials (Mn/Ni/Co sulfates), semiconductor inputs (gallium, ~15 t/yr), heat-resistant alloys (Ulba Metallurgical Plant), and permanent-magnet recycling (Nd/Dy). Partnerships: a 2022 EU raw-materials/battery deal (CRMA targets), a $200M Creada–HMS Bergbau lithium JV, and Tau-Ken Samruk exploration in Rwanda/Afghanistan. Headwinds: capital intensity (10-yr lead times) and fears of Russian/Chinese monopolization.

## 6. Semiconductors / Microelectronics
- **Plan:** Emerging via academia/JV — "One Student, One Chip" + Suto Kazakhstan plant *(no national Chip Act)*
- **Body:** Ministry of Science and Higher Education / Nazarbayev University / Suto Kazakhstan (JV)
- **Year:** 2024–2025
- **URL:** https://nu.edu.kz
- **Summary:** Explicitly nascent — no multi-billion-dollar Chip Act. The strategy avoids the "fab-first trap," focusing on fabless design and verification (Nazarbayev University). In 2024 NU adopted the Chinese Academy of Sciences' **"One Student, One Chip"** program (students design open-source RISC-V processors), yielding Kazakhstan's first locally designed microchip in 2025 — IP domestic, fabrication outsourced to a Chinese partner via Multi-Project Wafer. This spawned startups like TEXER.AI (EDA/verification, backed by QazInnovation grants), aiming to provide IP/verification services globally without a domestic cleanroom. On physical manufacturing, a $50M chip/electronics plant began construction in 2025 in the Abai region (Ondiris zone) via **Suto Kazakhstan** (Kazakh-Chinese JV, part of a $1.5B bilateral drive) — phased through 2028, producing lower-to-mid-tier microchips, optoelectronic modules, controllers, and displays.

## 7. Digital Technology / Digital Infrastructure
- **Plan:** State Program "Digital Kazakhstan" + Concept of Digital Transformation 2023–2029; Digital Code of Kazakhstan
- **Body:** Government (MDDIAI) / President / Parliament
- **Year:** 2017 (Digital Kazakhstan); 2026 (Digital Code, in force July 2026)
- **URL:** https://www.gov.kz / https://adilet.zan.kz
- **Summary:** The most mature, aggressively-funded domain. **Digital Kazakhstan** (2017) was built on five vectors (digitizing industry, e-government, "Digital Silk Road" data infrastructure, human capital, innovation), establishing **Astana Hub** (Central Asia's largest IT startup hub) and a vast **eGov** portal (eGovBusiness alone handles 4.6M+ operations/yr). It evolved into "Digital Kazakhstan 2.0" (Concept 2023–2029), backed by an ADB $10B regional fund and $280M (2024) for startups. The **Digital Code** (signed Jan 2026, in force July 2026) is among the world's first — a seven-section framework establishing digital human rights (Article 5, protection from arbitrary algorithmic restrictions, right to "digital identity"), data-control rights (deletion/anonymization via a personal "digital wallet"), legally normalized everyday "digital confirmations" (SMS/biometrics/OTP), and mandated accessibility — operating in tandem with the AI Law.

## 8. Autonomous Systems
- **Plan:** Rules for the Operation of Unmanned Aerial Vehicles *(no national drone-manufacturing strategy)*
- **Body:** Civil Aviation Committee (CAC), Ministry of Industry and Infrastructure Development; Aviation Administration of Kazakhstan (AAK)
- **Year:** Continuous updates
- **URL:** https://www.gov.kz
- **Summary:** Drones are tightly regulated for airspace safety (no visionary national drone strategy). The CAC (with the AAK, and collaborating with JARUS) requires registration of drones above weight thresholds (historically >0.25 kg high-risk, >1.5 kg generally) via the e-government portal (up to 10 working days). Flight rules: strictly VLOS, ≤120 m altitude, no night flights, ≥150 m from crowds and ≥50 m from vehicles/buildings on takeoff, and bans within 10 km of airports or over military/government infrastructure without multi-agency clearance. Commercial operators need a Kazakhstan drone-pilot license and liability insurance. Domestic production is nascent — educational robotics in schools and regional drone-operator training (e.g., Turkistan), but no prominent industrial/military drone manufacturing; policy favors safe consumer integration over export ambitions.
