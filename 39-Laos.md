# Laos (Lao PDR) — National Technology Strategies (8 Domains)

*Source: Gemini deep research, 2026. Entity 39 (Batch 8). ASEAN.*

Laos is attempting to pivot from a resource-dependent, landlocked economy (the hydropower "Battery of Southeast Asia") to a "land-linked," digitally integrated ASEAN hub, now anchored in the new **10th National Socio-Economic Development Plan 2026–2030** (endorsed by the National Assembly in November 2025, succeeding the 9th NSEDP 2021–2025) and the **20-Year National Digital Economy Development Vision 2021–2040**. Technological readiness is sharply bifurcated: digital infrastructure, AI, and critical-mineral extraction have active policy/legislation, while quantum and semiconductors are entirely absent. A defining feature is heavy **geopolitical dependency** — nuclear on Russia (Rosatom); rare earths, potash, and digital infrastructure on China (Belt and Road / Digital Silk Road) — amplified by high national debt (incl. $3.23B deferred debt to China). Laos aims to graduate from LDC status by 2026, and the 10th NSEDP's "self-reliant, knowledge-based, green economy" motto (targeting ~6% annual GDP growth) explicitly folds technology — including a National AI Strategy, digital transformation, and a nuclear pivot — into its post-graduation upper-middle-income agenda.

## 1. Nuclear Energy
- **Plan:** Russia–Laos Intergovernmental Agreement (IGA) on Cooperation in the Peaceful Use of Atomic Energy; preceded by the 2025–2026 Roadmap and earlier MoUs (2016, 2017) *(no nuclear power plants yet)*
- **Body:** Ministry of Industry and Commerce (MOIC) / Ministry of Energy and Mines (MEM), bilaterally with Russia's Rosatom
- **Year:** IGA signed 15 June 2026 (Moscow); Roadmap signed August 2025
- **URL:** https://moic.gov.la / https://mem.gov.la / https://rosatom.ru
- **Summary:** Despite hydropower producing ~76–80% of domestic electricity, Laos has formalized a nuclear pivot to diversify against climate-induced hydrological risk and boost cross-border electricity exports (Thailand, Cambodia, potentially Singapore). On 15 June 2026, during PM Sonexay Siphandone's state visit to Moscow, Rosatom Director General Alexey Likhachev and Lao Minister of Industry and Commerce Malaithong Kommasith signed a framework **intergovernmental agreement** (witnessed by PMs Mishustin and Sonexay) establishing the legal structure to build a Russian-designed plant — a significant escalation from the August 2025 Roadmap (itself signed during President Thongloun Sisoulith's Moscow visit). The IGA's first phase is a **preliminary feasibility study** to assess integrating nuclear into the hydropower-dominated grid and identify optimal sites and configurations, with Rosatom explicitly offering **Small Modular Reactor (SMR)** technology (floating and land-based), mirroring its parallel Southeast Asian pushes in Myanmar and Indonesia. The agenda also spans non-energy applications (medical radioisotopes, food-irradiation) and workforce training. On regulation, a 2016 Lao MoH–Rostechnadzor agreement seeded a radiation-safety framework; in April 2024 Laos ratified the A/CPPNM amendment and adopted the IAEA Code of Conduct on Radioactive Sources. Deployment still faces severe constraints — geology, cooling water, transmission upgrades — and no construction has begun.

## 2. Artificial Intelligence
- **Plan:** National Artificial Intelligence Strategy (officially launched), guided by the UNESCO AI Ethics Readiness Assessment Methodology (RAM) Country Report
- **Body:** Ministry of Technology and Communications (MTC), in partnership with UNESCO
- **Year:** Officially announced 27 January 2026 (Vientiane National Workshop on AI); earlier draft from January 2025
- **URL:** https://mtc.gov.la / https://www.unesco.org / https://kpl.gov.la
- **Summary:** AI moved from draft to a formally announced national strategy on **27 January 2026** at the National Workshop on Artificial Intelligence in Vientiane, where Minister Santisouk Simmalavong framed it as a "blueprint" for homegrown AI to modernize governance, education, agriculture, tourism, and culture, bridge the digital divide, and establish a national **AI Data Center**. The strategy was shaped by the UNESCO RAM Country Report, which diagnosed weak human capacity, institutional coordination, and R&D investment (Laos ranked 125th on the Oxford Insights Government AI Readiness Index, with limited private-sector and university research). In a concrete deployment step, the MTC launched **G-Chat v2** in April 2026 integrating **LaoAI** — a bespoke national AI assistant for civil servants (document summarization, drafting, real-time regulatory knowledge) — operationalizing the strategy's "homegrown" emphasis. Sectoral focus spans e-government (multilingual citizen services, data-driven policy), agriculture (Lao-language voice advice, weather/market platforms), and education/tourism. To compensate for capacity gaps, Laos signed a 2026 MoU with three Chinese entities (incl. the China-ASEAN Information Harbor) to deploy healthcare AI under Belt and Road. Specific budget is unpublicized.

## 3. Quantum Information Science
- **Plan:** *Explicitly absent* — no national quantum strategy, law, or policy
- **Body:** N/A
- **Year:** N/A
- **URL:** N/A
- **Summary:** Laos has no official strategy, plan, or policy dedicated to Quantum Information Science and Technology. Unlike Singapore (CQT/SHINE) or Vietnam (exploring quantum-resistant encryption), its technological baseline has not reached the quantum frontier. Laos occasionally appears in broader ASEAN/global consortium discussions, but quantum is entirely absent from the 9th NSEDP and the National Digital Economy Vision 2021–2040. Priorities remain foundational 4IR architecture (5G, basic AI, cloud data centers) rather than deep-tech quantum research.

## 4. Biomedical Sciences / Biotechnology
- **Plan:** Decision on the Management, Processing, Production, and Use of Biopharmaceutical Products, Genes, and Stem Cells (Decision No. 3730/MOH)
- **Body:** Ministry of Health (MOH); biodiversity via Biotechnology and Ecology Institute (BEI), Ministry of Science and Technology
- **Year:** 22 November 2024 (effective 18 January 2025)
- **URL:** https://moh.gov.la
- **Summary:** Decision No. 3730/MOH is a landmark giving formal legal recognition to biopharmaceuticals, gene therapies, and stem cells for both medical treatment and cosmetic applications — with strict definitions (biopharmaceuticals as biological products from natural substances; gene therapy via DNA/RNA manipulation; stem cells by undifferentiated self-renewal). It mandates facility standards, quality control, and ethical sourcing; prohibits importing embryo/fetus-derived raw materials; and requires enterprise registration, production licenses, and quality certifications under the Law on Drugs and Medical Products. On biodiversity, Laos is an active party to the Nagoya Protocol on Access and Benefit Sharing — using Material Transfer Agreements to protect its genetic resources (plants, animals, microorganisms) from unregulated bioprospecting and ensure equitable benefits, with BEI building mycological/genetic capacity.

## 5. Materials Science / Critical Raw Materials
- **Plan:** MOIC Five-Year Plan 2026–2030 (incorporating rare-earth management and the Decree on Trial Mining/Processing/Export); Law on Minerals (2017, under revision)
- **Body:** Ministry of Industry and Commerce (MOIC) / Ministry of Energy and Mines (MEM) / Department of Geology and Mines
- **Year:** Second draft of MOIC 2026–2030 plan released 7 September 2025; trial decrees 2023–2025; Law 2017
- **URL:** https://moic.gov.la / https://mem.gov.la
- **Summary:** Mining is an economic backbone (~12% of government revenue, ~10% of national income, >50% of exports). Following environmental controversies and unchecked rare-earth expansion, Laos has moved to a more interventionist posture: the **MOIC Five-Year Plan 2026–2030** (second draft released 7 September 2025) integrates comprehensive rare-earth management — geological mapping, tightened legal frameworks, and mandatory **in-country/downstream processing** (e.g. Lao-China smelting plants) to add value before export, ending raw-resource exportation of rare earths, iron ore, and raw gold. By mid-2026, the Department of Geology and Mines issued a stream of compliance directives and mandated national laboratories to analyze and oversee precious-metal/REE exports, aiming to curb financial leakage and Mekong-basin pollution. The MEM still groups minerals into five strategic categories, prioritizing high-volume **potash** (est. 133.6 billion tons of potassium chloride — aiming to make Laos Asia's primary potash-fertilizer hub via the China-Laos Railway; >$1B Chinese investment, e.g. Asia-Potash, in Khammouane) and **Heavy Rare Earth Elements** (ion-adsorption clays rich in dysprosium/terbium in Xieng Khouang, Houaphanh, Bolaven Plateau). Governance remains problematic — weak institutional capacity and foreign-debt leverage ($3.23B deferred to China) limit enforcement autonomy; the Feb 2024 Nam Sam River chemical leak (cyanide, arsenic, lead) and potash-mining sinkholes in Vientiane province (prompting a National Assembly survey suspension) illustrate the blowback the new regulations target. Germany's BGR assists oversight via the SEPM project.

## 6. Semiconductors / Microelectronics
- **Plan:** *Nascent / policy absent* — no sovereign semiconductor strategy
- **Body:** N/A
- **Year:** N/A
- **URL:** N/A
- **Summary:** Laos has no national strategy, law, or roadmap for semiconductor manufacturing, IC design, or microelectronics. Its ecosystem and human capital are insufficient for fabrication — roughly one engineer per 5,000–7,000 people, far behind Vietnam, Malaysia, and Singapore. Yet Laos isn't fully disconnected: 2024 data shows ~$410M in "semiconductor device" exports (to the US, India, Thailand) and $32.5M imports (from China, Cambodia) — likely low-end assembly, rudimentary testing, or transshipment by foreign-owned subsidiaries leveraging low labor costs rather than indigenous capability. Under the ASEAN Business Advisory Council's proposed AFISS framework, Laos (with Cambodia/Myanmar) is positioned for a peripheral, supportive role: logistics, vocational training, and supplying renewable hydropower to energy-intensive semiconductor operations in neighboring states.

## 7. Digital Technology / Digital Infrastructure
- **Plan:** National Digital Transformation Plan 2026–2030 (superseding the 2021–2025 Digital Economy Plan); 20-Year Digital Economy Development Vision 2021–2040; Law on Cybersecurity (gazetted 2026)
- **Body:** Ministry of Technology and Communications (MTC) / National Digital Transformation Committee (NDTC)
- **Year:** 2026–2030 Plan finalized 27 January 2026; Cybersecurity Law gazetted 20 March 2026; Vision December 2021
- **URL:** https://mtc.gov.la
- **Summary:** The most comprehensively planned domain — central to transitioning Laos from "land-locked" to "land-linked," with the Vision 2040 still targeting growth of the digital economy from ~3% to **10% of GDP by 2040**. Chaired by PM Sonexay Siphandone, the government declared **2026 the "decisive year for digital transformation,"** finalizing (27 January 2026, for National Assembly submission) a new **National Digital Transformation Plan 2026–2030** that supersedes the 2021–2025 framework and shifts focus to full e-government integration, interoperable cross-border payments (LAPNet / ASEAN QR), and piloting the Bank of the Lao PDR's central bank digital currency (DLak). Laos marked its first **National Digital Day** on 10 January 2026 (10/01, symbolizing binary), reporting 4G coverage of ~76% of villages and a fiber-optic backbone exceeding 36,000 km; the MTC also launched an anti-fraud "1533" Online Scam Centre and a June 2026 UNDP public-sector digitalization project. The **Law on Cybersecurity** was published in the Official Gazette on 20 March 2026, identifying five critical national information-infrastructure sectors (defense/security, tech/comms, finance, energy, commerce/logistics), aligning data privacy with international standards, mandating annual cybersecurity reporting, and operating a National Cybersecurity Operations System with big-data/AI analytics. Funding remains heavily China-dependent — a flagship national data center and high-speed network with the China International Development Cooperation Agency ties Lao digital architecture to the Digital Silk Road.

## 8. Autonomous Systems
- **Plan:** Regulations on Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAV) / Drone Laws *(no standalone robotics strategy)*
- **Body:** Department of Civil Aviation (DCA), Ministry of Public Works and Transport; Ministry of Post and Telecommunications
- **Year:** Actively enforced (current 2025/2026 guidelines)
- **URL:** https://mpwt.gov.la
- **Summary:** Split domain. **Drones (UAS)** are legal but strictly governed by the DCA per localized ICAO guidelines, for airspace safety, security, and UNESCO-heritage protection. Requirements scale by weight: micro (<100–200 g) generally need no registration; small/recreational (>200 g–1 kg) must register with MPWT/MPT; commercial/large (>1 kg) need certification, commercial licensing, and location-specific permits (plus Radio Frequency Dept clearance). Operational limits: max 120 m (400 ft) altitude, VLOS within ~300 m, no flying within 5 km of airports or near military/government sites/crowds. Enforcement is real — commercial permits can take 3–4 weeks. **Robotics/automation** has no standalone policy, though the MTC frames robotics (with IoT and AI) as a core 4IR component; the private sector pushes Robotic Process Automation (projections that ~30% of business tasks could be automated). No state funding is earmarked for autonomous-systems research or domestic robotics manufacturing.
