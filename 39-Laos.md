# Laos (Lao PDR) — National Technology Strategies (8 Domains)

*Source: Gemini deep research, 2026. Entity 39 (Batch 8). ASEAN.*

Laos is attempting to pivot from a resource-dependent, landlocked economy (the hydropower "Battery of Southeast Asia") to a "land-linked," digitally integrated ASEAN hub, anchored in the **9th National Socio-Economic Development Plan 2021–2025** and the **20-Year National Digital Economy Development Vision 2021–2040**. Technological readiness is sharply bifurcated: digital infrastructure, AI, and critical-mineral extraction have active policy/legislation, while quantum and semiconductors are entirely absent. A defining feature is heavy **geopolitical dependency** — nuclear on Russia (Rosatom); rare earths, potash, and digital infrastructure on China (Belt and Road / Digital Silk Road) — amplified by high national debt (incl. $3.23B deferred debt to China). Laos aims to graduate from LDC status by 2026.

## 1. Nuclear Energy
- **Plan:** Roadmap for Cooperation in the Peaceful Use of Nuclear Energy (2025–2026); earlier MoUs (2016, 2017) *(no nuclear power plants yet)*
- **Body:** Ministry of Energy and Mines (MEM) / Ministry of Industry and Commerce (MOIC), bilaterally with Russia's Rosatom
- **Year:** Signed July/August 2025
- **URL:** https://mem.gov.la / https://moic.gov.la
- **Summary:** Despite hydropower producing ~76–80% of domestic electricity, Laos has formalized a nuclear pivot to diversify against climate-induced hydrological risk and boost cross-border electricity exports (Thailand, Cambodia, potentially Singapore). The 2025–2026 Roadmap (signed during President Thongloun Sisoulith's Moscow visit) targets: (1) building Laos's first nuclear plant — Russian VVER technology, explicitly considering SMRs to fit the small national grid (earlier 2015 talks envisioned two 1,000 MWe BOT reactors); (2) non-energy applications (medical radioisotopes, food-irradiation); and (3) nuclear workforce training. On regulation, a 2016 Lao MoH–Rostechnadzor agreement seeded a radiation-safety framework; in April 2024 Laos ratified the A/CPPNM amendment and adopted the IAEA Code of Conduct on Radioactive Sources. Funding sits within Rosatom's ~$200.4B overseas portfolio (Rosatom leads 2025–26 feasibility/siting). Deployment faces severe constraints — geology, cooling water, transmission upgrades.

## 2. Artificial Intelligence
- **Plan:** (Draft) Lao National Artificial Intelligence Strategy, guided by the UNESCO AI Ethics Readiness Assessment Methodology (RAM) Country Report
- **Body:** Ministry of Technology and Communications (MTC)
- **Year:** Announced January 2025; drafting into 2026
- **URL:** https://mtc.gov.la (with unesco.org collaboration)
- **Summary:** AI is being formalized as a state-policy pillar under the 9th NSEDP's digital-transformation mandate. The draft strategy aims to "enable AI as a key driver for digital transformation… based on responsible and ethical AI," prioritizing "homegrown" solutions that respect cultural identity. Sectoral focus: e-government (multilingual citizen services, data-driven policy), agriculture (Lao-language voice advice, weather/market platforms for rural farmers), and education/tourism (personalized learning, culturally-aware chatbots). The 2025/26 Lao PDR RAM Country Report (with UNESCO) diagnosed weak human capacity, institutional coordination, and R&D investment; Laos ranked 125th on the Oxford Insights Government AI Readiness Index, with limited private-sector (e.g., startup Laligence) and university research. To compensate, Laos signed a 2026 MoU with three Chinese entities (incl. the China-ASEAN Information Harbor) to deploy healthcare AI under Belt and Road (public-health management, disease analysis, medical-record digitization). Specific budget is unpublicized.

## 3. Quantum Information Science
- **Plan:** *Explicitly absent* — no national quantum strategy, law, or policy
- **Body:** N/A
- **Year:** N/A
- **URL:** N/A
- **Summary:** Laos has no official strategy, plan, or policy dedicated to Quantum Information Science and Technology. Unlike Singapore (CQT/SHINE) or Vietnam (exploring quantum-resistant encryption), its technological baseline has not reached the quantum frontier. Laos occasionally appears in broader ASEAN/global consortium discussions, but quantum is entirely absent from the 9th NSEDP and the National Digital Economy Vision 2021–2040. Priorities remain foundational 4IR architecture (5G, basic AI, cloud data centers) rather than deep-tech quantum research.

## 4. Biomedical Sciences / Biotechnology
- **Plan:** Decision on the Management, Processing, Production, and Use of Biopharmaceutical Products, Genes, and Stem Cells (Decision No. 3730/MOH)
- **Body:** Ministry of Health (MOH); biodiversity via Biotechnology and Ecology Institute (BEI), Ministry of Science and Technology
- **Year:** 22 November 2024 (effective 18 January 2025)
- **URL:** https://moh.gov.la
- **Summary:** Decision No. 3730/MOH is a landmark giving formal legal recognition to biopharmaceuticals, gene therapies, and stem cells for both medical treatment and cosmetic applications — with strict definitions (biopharmaceuticals as biological products from natural substances; gene therapy via DNA/RNA manipulation; stem cells by undifferentiated self-renewal). It mandates facility standards, quality control, and ethical sourcing; prohibits importing embryo/fetus-derived raw materials; and requires enterprise registration, production licenses, and quality certifications under the Law on Drugs and Medical Products. On biodiversity, Laos is an active party to the Nagoya Protocol on Access and Benefit Sharing — using Material Transfer Agreements to protect its genetic resources (plants, animals, microorganisms) from unregulated bioprospecting and ensure equitable benefits, with BEI building mycological/genetic capacity.

## 5. Materials Science / Critical Raw Materials
- **Plan:** Law on Minerals (2017, under revision); Decree on Trial Mining; Five-Year Plan for the Industry and Commerce Sector
- **Body:** Ministry of Energy and Mines (MEM) / Department of Mining Management (DMM)
- **Year:** 2017 (Law); trial decrees 2023–2025
- **URL:** https://mem.gov.la
- **Summary:** Mining is an economic backbone (~12% of government revenue, ~10% of national income, >50% of exports). The MEM groups minerals into five strategic categories, prioritizing high-volume **potash** (est. 133.6 billion tons of potassium chloride — aiming to make Laos Asia's primary potash-fertilizer hub via the China-Laos Railway; >$1B Chinese investment, e.g. Asia-Potash, in Khammouane) and **Heavy Rare Earth Elements** (ion-adsorption clays rich in dysprosium/terbium in Xieng Khouang, Houaphanh, Bolaven Plateau). Chinese REE processors expanded into northern Laos as China tightened its own rules and Myanmar destabilized; Laos established the first Lao-China Rare Earth Research Centre and granted 20+ exploration permits via trial-mining decrees. Governance is highly problematic — weak institutional capacity and foreign-debt leverage ($3.23B deferred to China) limit enforcement autonomy. Severe environmental blowback includes potash-mining sinkholes/subsidence in Vientiane province (prompting a National Assembly suspension of new surveys) and a Feb 2024 chemical leak from a Chinese-Lao REE mine that contaminated the Nam Sam River (cyanide, arsenic, lead). Germany's BGR assists oversight via the SEPM project.

## 6. Semiconductors / Microelectronics
- **Plan:** *Nascent / policy absent* — no sovereign semiconductor strategy
- **Body:** N/A
- **Year:** N/A
- **URL:** N/A
- **Summary:** Laos has no national strategy, law, or roadmap for semiconductor manufacturing, IC design, or microelectronics. Its ecosystem and human capital are insufficient for fabrication — roughly one engineer per 5,000–7,000 people, far behind Vietnam, Malaysia, and Singapore. Yet Laos isn't fully disconnected: 2024 data shows ~$410M in "semiconductor device" exports (to the US, India, Thailand) and $32.5M imports (from China, Cambodia) — likely low-end assembly, rudimentary testing, or transshipment by foreign-owned subsidiaries leveraging low labor costs rather than indigenous capability. Under the ASEAN Business Advisory Council's proposed AFISS framework, Laos (with Cambodia/Myanmar) is positioned for a peripheral, supportive role: logistics, vocational training, and supplying renewable hydropower to energy-intensive semiconductor operations in neighboring states.

## 7. Digital Technology / Digital Infrastructure
- **Plan:** 20-Year National Digital Economy Development Vision (2021–2040); 10-Year Strategy (2021–2030); 5-Year Plan (2021–2025); Law on Cybersecurity (2024)
- **Body:** Ministry of Technology and Communications (MTC)
- **Year:** Vision/Strategy December 2021; Cybersecurity Law 2024
- **URL:** https://mtc.gov.la
- **Summary:** The most comprehensively planned domain — central to transitioning Laos from "land-locked" to "land-linked." The Vision 2040's headline target is to grow the digital economy from ~3% to **10% of GDP by 2040**. The current 5-Year Plan focuses on foundations: expanding rural broadband and 5G (launched 2024); coverage claims of 97% (2G), 85% (3G), 78% (4G). e-Government (Gov-X) builds a central data-exchange platform (GDX) for inter-ministerial interoperability, a National Digital ID, and integration of fragmented portals (TaxRIS, ASYCUDA). Smart Cities (Vientiane 450, Amata Namor) and border Dry Ports (with China/Vietnam/Thailand) target regional e-commerce. The 2024 Law on Cybersecurity (with a 2035 National Cyber Security Development Strategic Plan) makes cybersecurity a top priority, requiring critical-infrastructure entities to connect to a National Cybersecurity Operations System and digital-service providers to register/license locally. Funding is heavily China-dependent — a flagship national data center and high-speed network with the China International Development Cooperation Agency ties Lao digital architecture to the Digital Silk Road.

## 8. Autonomous Systems
- **Plan:** Regulations on Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAV) / Drone Laws *(no standalone robotics strategy)*
- **Body:** Department of Civil Aviation (DCA), Ministry of Public Works and Transport; Ministry of Post and Telecommunications
- **Year:** Actively enforced (current 2025/2026 guidelines)
- **URL:** https://mpwt.gov.la
- **Summary:** Split domain. **Drones (UAS)** are legal but strictly governed by the DCA per localized ICAO guidelines, for airspace safety, security, and UNESCO-heritage protection. Requirements scale by weight: micro (<100–200 g) generally need no registration; small/recreational (>200 g–1 kg) must register with MPWT/MPT; commercial/large (>1 kg) need certification, commercial licensing, and location-specific permits (plus Radio Frequency Dept clearance). Operational limits: max 120 m (400 ft) altitude, VLOS within ~300 m, no flying within 5 km of airports or near military/government sites/crowds. Enforcement is real — commercial permits can take 3–4 weeks. **Robotics/automation** has no standalone policy, though the MTC frames robotics (with IoT and AI) as a core 4IR component; the private sector pushes Robotic Process Automation (projections that ~30% of business tasks could be automated). No state funding is earmarked for autonomous-systems research or domestic robotics manufacturing.
